# Session Changes — 2026-03-24

## Context

A DeBERTa-v3 classifier trained on the synthetic dataset achieved an unrealistically high F1 of ~0.99 on emotion classification. Root cause: the generator was producing complaints with strong, learnable patterns that made emotion trivially detectable — not a model problem, but a data quality problem.

Three fixes were applied across two files.

---

## Fix 1 — Style-emotion affinity removed (`prompts.py`)

**Before:** `STYLE_EMOTION` restricted which emotion levels each writing style could appear at:
- `"Formal professional"` → Low, Medium only
- `"Passive-aggressive / sarcastic"` → Medium, High only
- `"Polite but firm"` → Low, Medium only
- All other styles → Low, Medium, High

Style was therefore a near-perfect proxy for emotion. A classifier could predict emotion from style alone.

**After:** All 8 styles are allowed at all 3 emotion levels (Low, Medium, High). Real angry customers sometimes write formally (cold fury); real calm customers can be subtly sarcastic.

---

## Fix 2 — CRITICAL TONE instructions softened (`generate_complaints.py`)

**Before:** `EMOTION_INSTRUCTIONS` told the model exactly what vocabulary to use per emotion level:
- Low: "Avoid words like 'frustrated', 'disappointed', 'unacceptable'"
- Medium: "Use language like 'frustrated', 'disappointed', 'unacceptable', 'fed up'"
- High: "Use strong emotional language — 'appalling', 'disgusted', 'livid', 'disgraceful', 'absolutely furious'. Include threats to escalate to Ofcom."

This created a near-perfect vocabulary lookup table. DeBERTa could hit 0.99 F1 by learning these word lists without understanding the text.

**After:** Instructions suppress explicit emotion labels and formatting cues, and instead ask the model to convey emotion through word choice and sentence structure:
- Low: no exclamation marks, no capitalisation, no emotion-announcing words; one understated expression of disappointment is acceptable
- Medium: frustration should be sensed, not announced; ambiguity between calm and irritated is intentional
- High: explicitly offers the "cold controlled anger vs explicit distress" fork based on style and profile; bans reliance on exclamation marks, capitalisation, or words like 'livid'/'furious'/'appalling'

---

## Fix 3 — Emotion level definitions made to overlap (`prompts.py`)

**Before:** `EMOTION_DEFINITIONS` described three clearly separated registers:
- Low: "reads like a matter-of-fact report" — zero emotional content
- Medium: specific signal words listed ('frustrating', 'unacceptable', 'fed up')
- High: strong adjectives listed ('appalling', 'disgraceful', 'absolutely livid'), threats to Ofcom

The boundaries were sharp, leaving no ambiguity between classes.

**After:** Definitions overlap at the margins:
- Low: "largely in check, though a single understated expression of disappointment or weariness may appear"
- Medium: "could be read as either controlled irritation or mild emotional expression depending on context" — explicitly ambiguous
- High: defined as a range (cold/formal anger OR explicit distress), not a single register; emotion should be "felt, not announced"
