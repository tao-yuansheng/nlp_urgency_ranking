# Contrastive Error Analysis: Executive Summary Table

Here is a synthesized summary table of the algorithmic blindspots and actionable dataset strategies:

| Core Algorithmic Blindspot | Description | Actionable Dataset Strategy |
| :------------------------- | :---------- | :-------------------------- |
| **1. Emotional & Vulnerability Over-indexing / De-escalation Under-weighting** | The model frequently misinterprets expressions of emotional distress, personal vulnerability, or general frustration as higher urgency, while failing to adequately weigh explicit statements of patience, de-escalation, or willingness to wait. | Augment training data with more examples where emotional language, vulnerability, or frustration are present but the overall context or explicit de-escalation cues warrant a 'Low' or 'Medium' urgency. |
| **2. Contextual Misinterpretation of Deadlines & Threats** | The model either over-prioritizes or under-weights explicit deadlines, external escalation threats (e.g., Ombudsman, legal action), and high-urgency keywords ('immediate', 'priority') without sufficient contextualization of the actual impact, immediacy of the threat, or the underlying issue's criticality. | Add more training examples that clearly differentiate urgency levels when explicit deadlines, threats, or strong keywords are present, ensuring they are contextualized by the actual impact and resolution timeframe. Include examples of 'Low' urgency with strong language/threats, and 'Medium' urgency with specific, short deadlines. |
| **3. Failure to Synthesize Problem History & Cumulative Impact** | The model struggles to weigh the cumulative effect of repeated service failures, prolonged unresolved issues, or ongoing administrative burden. It often over-indexes on single, recent events or 'first contact' statements, diminishing the perceived urgency of persistent problems. | Augment training data with more examples that clearly link a history of repeated failures, unfulfilled promises, or ongoing disruption to escalating urgency ('Medium' or 'High'), even without explicit, immediate threats. Differentiate between single past events and ongoing crises. |
| **4. Inaccurate Severity & Impact Differentiation** | The model struggles to distinguish between different levels of impact (e.g., significant inconvenience vs. critical, life-threatening, or rapidly escalating threats) and types of impact (e.g., personal financial strain vs. business livelihood), especially when strong language is used for both. | Add more training examples that clearly differentiate between 'Medium' and 'High' urgency when strong impact language (health, safety, financial, business disruption) is present, ensuring the model distinguishes between significant disruption and immediate, life-critical threats. |
| **5. Over-reliance on Explicit Demands vs. Inferred Urgency** | The model prioritizes direct, explicit demands, quantifiable impacts, and assertive language, failing to infer urgency from underlying critical circumstances (e.g., health crises, disability) when the customer's language is polite, indirect, or focused on a first step in a process. | Augment training data with more examples where underlying critical circumstances (e.g., health, safety, essential services) drive 'High' or 'Medium' urgency, even if the customer's language is polite, an inquiry, or lacks explicit temporal markers. |
| **6. Conflating Procedural Escalation with Criticality** | The model misinterprets formal or procedural escalation language (e.g., 'exhausted standard channels', 'Ombudsman threat as a standard next step) as inherently high urgency, regardless of the underlying issue's actual criticality or the specified resolution timeframe. | Augment training data with more examples where formal escalation cues are present but the overall context (e.g., nature of the problem, resolution timeline) still warrants a 'Low' or 'Medium' urgency, to teach the model to differentiate procedural escalation from immediate crisis. |
